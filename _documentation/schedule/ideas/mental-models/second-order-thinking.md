# Second-Order Thinking

First-order thinking asks "what happens?" Second-order thinking asks "and then what?"

Most people stop at the immediate consequence. Second-order thinkers trace the chain.

## When to use
- Policy/product decisions: what behavior does this incentivize downstream?
- Competitive strategy: if we do X, what will competitors do, and what does that change?
- Personal decisions: what does saying yes to this mean for the next 6 months?

## Example
First-order: "Discounting our price will get more customers."
Second-order: "More customers at lower margin = more support load, lower revenue per user, trains customers to wait for discounts."

First-order: "I'll grind this weekend to ship faster."
Second-order: "I'll be 30% less effective next week, and the habit will compound."

## Pitfall
Analysis paralysis. Second-order thinking is for important decisions, not every micro-choice. Pick the depth of thinking based on stakes.
