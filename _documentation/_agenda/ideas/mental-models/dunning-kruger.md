# Dunning-Kruger Effect

People with low competence in a domain tend to overestimate their ability. People with high competence tend to underestimate it (because they know what they don't know).

The curve: confident beginner → humbled intermediate → quietly confident expert.

## When to use (watch for it)
- When you feel very certain about something you haven't spent much time on
- When calibrating how much to trust someone's opinion in an unfamiliar domain
- When you're hiring or evaluating people — the most confident candidate isn't always the most skilled

## The useful insight
The "valley of despair" (feeling incompetent after the initial peak) is a signal of real learning, not failure. If you've stopped feeling confident, you may be making real progress.

## Example
Junior dev who's been coding 3 months is certain their architecture is production-ready. Senior dev with 10 years wants 3 more reviews before shipping. The senior knows how much can go wrong.

## Pitfall
Dunning-Kruger is often misquoted as "dumb people think they're smart." It's more nuanced: it's about domain-specific calibration, and it affects everyone in new domains.
